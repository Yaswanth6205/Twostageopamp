# Twostageopamp Design and implementation of two stage operational amplifier with miller compensation and nulling resistor

90nm CMOS Two-Stage Miller Compensated Op-Amp

This repository contains the design, mathematical modeling, and Cadence Virtuoso simulation results for a high-performance, low-voltage Two-Stage Operational Amplifier. The project benchmarks a custom 90nm CMOS design against the industry-standard IC 741, demonstrating a 1000x improvement in power efficiency[cite: 67, 70, 1013].

Project Specifications

  • Technology Node: 90nm CMOS (gpdk090) [cite: 63, 845]
  • Supply Voltage: 1.2V [cite: 64, 203]
  • DC Gain: 60 dB [cite: 69, 204, 978]
  • Unity Gain Bandwidth (UGB): 23 MHz [cite: 69, 205, 981]
  • Phase Margin: 74 degrees [cite: 69, 206, 982]
  • Power Consumption: 108 micro-watts [cite: 70, 208, 929]

Architecture and Design Methodology

1. Why a Two-Stage Architecture?

A single-stage amplifier cannot simultaneously achieve high gain and large output swing in 90nm technology due to reduced voltage headroom[cite: 157, 158]. This design separates these functions: Stage 1 (Differential Pair) provides high gain and common-mode rejection[cite: 169, 618], while Stage 2 (Common-Source) provides additional gain and a large output swing[cite: 170, 629].

2. Miller Compensation and Pole Splitting

Two-stage amplifiers are inherently unstable because they introduce two significant poles[cite: 191, 229, 646]. Miller compensation uses a capacitor (Cc) between the input and output of the second stage to achieve Pole Splitting[cite: 275, 277]. This pushes the dominant pole (P1) to a lower frequency and the non-dominant pole (P2) to a higher frequency, ensuring stability[cite: 279, 659, 660].

3. The Nulling Resistor Solution

The Miller capacitor creates a feed-forward path that introduces a Right Half Plane (RHP) Zero[cite: 288, 290, 680]. This zero adds phase lag and degrades the phase margin[cite: 291, 689]. By placing a Nulling Resistor (Rz) in series with Cc and setting it approximately equal to 1/gm7, the zero is pushed to infinity or the left half plane to restore stability[cite: 292, 294, 698, 699].

Implementation in Cadence Virtuoso

The circuit was implemented using the gm/Id methodology to determine transistor dimensions analytically[cite: 784, 1039]. All simulations were performed in the Cadence Virtuoso environment[cite: 63, 845]:

  • DC Analysis: Verified that all transistors operate in the saturation region[cite: 837, 892, 923].
  • AC Analysis: Extracted the Bode plot to measure gain, bandwidth, and phase margin[cite: 900, 904, 931].
  • Transient Analysis: Measured the slew rate (8.05 V/micro-second) and settling behavior[cite: 905, 907, 989].
